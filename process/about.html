About Washington, DC

Brief history of DC

George Washington selected the site for Washington, DC, in 1791, combining land from Maryland and Virginia to establish a new capital separate from any state government.

The city was named after Washington, and the District of Columbia was named for Christopher Columbus. Early on, the capital was a small, planned city with broad avenues and important buildings like the Capitol and the White House.

During the Civil War and the years that followed, Washington, DC, grew quickly as a center of government, law, and military planning. Over time, more monuments, museums, and federal agencies were added, shaping the city we know today.

The district is not a state and has no voting representation in Congress. Congress controls the district’s laws and budget, although local residents gained an elected mayor and city council in the 1970s. Today, DC is both a national symbol and a city where people live, work, and organize for change.
